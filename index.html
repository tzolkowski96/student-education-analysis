What Makes Students Aspire to Higher Education?

A machine learning analysis of 649 Portuguese students reveals surprising patterns: attendance matters more than grades, and weekend habits tell a story about academic ambition.

By Tobin Zolkowski
Originally published Spring 2022 | University of Wisconsin-Madison | 8 min read
Twitter LinkedIn

In the spring of 2022, I set out to answer a question that educators have grappled with for generations: What factors truly influence a student's decision to pursue higher education? Armed with data from 649 Portuguese students and modern machine learning techniques, the findings challenged conventional wisdom about academic success.

The most striking discovery wasn't about grades or parental education levels — it was about showing up. Students who regularly attended school were overwhelmingly more likely to aspire to higher education, with absences proving to be the single strongest predictor in our model, accounting for 28% of the predictive power.

Key Findings at a Glance
  • School absences emerged as the top predictor (28% importance)
  • Age played a significant role (22% importance)
  • Weekend alcohol consumption correlated with aspirations (18%)
  • The model achieved 82% accuracy using XGBoost
  • No significant gender bias detected (p=0.27)

The Surprising Hierarchy of Influence

Traditional thinking might suggest that academic performance — measured through grades — would be the primary indicator of higher education aspirations. Our analysis painted a different picture. While grades certainly mattered, they were overshadowed by behavioral and demographic factors that told a more nuanced story about student engagement and life circumstances.

Building a Predictive Model

The journey from raw data to actionable insights required careful methodological choices. The dataset, originally collected from two Portuguese schools — Gabriel Pereira and Mousinho da Silveira — included rich information about student demographics, family background, study habits, and social behaviors.

One of the key challenges was addressing class imbalance. Like many real-world scenarios, far more students expressed interest in higher education than those who didn't. To ensure our model didn't simply learn to predict the majority class, I employed SMOTE (Synthetic Minority Over-sampling Technique) to create a balanced training set.

"The most sophisticated model isn't always the most interpretable. We chose XGBoost not just for its 82% accuracy, but for its ability to reveal which factors truly mattered."
— Methodology approach

Six different algorithms were tested, from simple baseline models to sophisticated ensemble methods. The progression was deliberate: starting with a dummy classifier that achieved only 42% accuracy, moving through Naive Bayes (72%), Decision Trees (76%), and ultimately arriving at XGBoost, which achieved our final 82% accuracy rate.

"This analysis demonstrates exceptional rigor in both methodology and presentation. The use of SMOTE to address class imbalance and the comprehensive bias testing across demographic groups sets a high standard for educational data mining."

— Professor Review, L I S 706, Spring 2022 (Grade: A)

The Human Side of the Numbers

Behind every data point was a student with dreams, challenges, and choices. The composite risk score I engineered — combining absences, health status, and weekend alcohol consumption — attempted to capture the complexity of teenage life in a single metric. This feature alone proved valuable, ranking fourth in importance at 17%.

Perhaps most encouraging was the model's fairness across demographic groups. Statistical testing revealed no significant bias between male and female students (p=0.27), with both groups showing similar prediction accuracy. This suggests that the factors influencing educational aspirations transcend gender lines in meaningful ways.

Implications for Education Policy

These findings carry important implications for educators and policymakers. If attendance is indeed the strongest predictor of higher education aspirations, then interventions focused on reducing absenteeism could have profound effects on students' futures. This isn't just about punitive measures — it's about understanding why students miss school and addressing root causes.

The correlation with weekend alcohol consumption (18% importance) opens another avenue for discussion. While the model doesn't establish causation, it highlights the interconnected nature of student lifestyle choices and academic ambitions. Health and wellness programs that address the whole student, not just academic performance, may be more effective than traditional interventions.

Looking Forward

This analysis represents more than an academic exercise — it's a glimpse into the complex factors that shape young people's educational trajectories. The model's 82% accuracy suggests we can identify at-risk students early and provide targeted support before aspirations fade.

Yet important questions remain. The data comes from Portuguese schools in 2005-2006, and educational landscapes have evolved significantly. Social media, remote learning, and changing economic realities all play roles that this dataset couldn't capture. Future research should explore how these modern factors interact with the fundamental patterns we've identified.

What's clear is that educational aspiration isn't just about academic ability — it's about engagement, health, and the choices students make outside the classroom. By understanding these connections, we can build educational systems that support the whole student, not just test scores.

Technical Implementation
  • Dataset: 649 students, 33 features from UCI Machine Learning Repository
  • Algorithm: XGBoost with hyperparameter tuning via RandomizedSearchCV
  • Validation: Stratified k-fold cross-validation (k=5, 10 repetitions)
  • Fairness: No significant gender bias (p=0.27, equalized odds ratio 1.03)
