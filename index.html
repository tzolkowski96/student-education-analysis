What Makes Students Aspire to Higher Education?

A machine learning analysis of 649 Portuguese students reveals surprising patterns: attendance matters more than grades, and weekend habits tell a story about academic ambition.

By Tobin Zolkowski
Originally published Spring 2022 | University of Wisconsin-Madison | 8 min read
Twitter LinkedIn

In the spring of 2022, I set out to answer a question that educators have grappled with for generations: What factors truly influence a student's decision to pursue higher education? Armed with data from 649 Portuguese students and modern machine learning techniques, the findings challenged conventional wisdom about academic success.

The most striking discovery wasn't about grades or parental education levels — it was about age and attendance. Older students were less likely to aspire to higher education, with age proving to be the single strongest predictor in our model.

Key Findings at a Glance
  • Age emerged as the top predictor (followed closely by absences)
  • School absences played a critical role (tipping point at 6 days)
  • Weekend alcohol consumption correlated with aspirations
  • The model achieved 77% accuracy using Random Forest
  • Male students showed lower likelihood of aspirations

The Surprising Hierarchy of Influence

Traditional thinking might suggest that academic performance — measured through grades — would be the primary indicator of higher education aspirations. Our analysis painted a different picture. While grades certainly mattered, they were overshadowed by behavioral and demographic factors that told a more nuanced story about student engagement and life circumstances.

The "Six-Day" Tipping Point

Digging deeper into the Random Forest analysis, we discovered a specific threshold that acts as a warning sign: six days.

Students with fewer than six absences were overwhelmingly likely to aspire to higher education. Once absences crossed this line, the probability dropped significantly. This suggests that attendance isn't just a compliance requirement—it's a barometer for a student's future outlook. It's not about "skipping school"; it's about disconnecting from the academic community.

The Weekend Paradox

Common wisdom suggests that "partying" hurts academic ambition. Our data tells a more complex, even counter-intuitive story.

Surprisingly, the model found that high weekend alcohol consumption didn't automatically disqualify students from having high aspirations. In fact, some students with higher consumption levels remained highly likely to aspire to university. This contradicts the simple assumption that social drinking equals academic disengagement. It suggests that for some students, social life and academic ambition coexist in a way that defies stereotypes.

Building a Predictive Model

The journey from raw data to actionable insights required careful methodological choices. The dataset, originally collected from two Portuguese schools — Gabriel Pereira and Mousinho da Silveira — included rich information about student demographics, family background, study habits, and social behaviors.

One of the key challenges was addressing class imbalance. Like many real-world scenarios, far more students expressed interest in higher education than those who didn't. To ensure our model didn't simply learn to predict the majority class, I employed SMOTE (Synthetic Minority Over-sampling Technique) to create a balanced training set.

Avoiding the "Average" Trap

In our dataset, most students wanted to go to college. If a computer simply guessed "Yes" for everyone, it would be right most of the time but learn nothing about the students who need help. This is called the "Metric Trap." We used a technique called SMOTE to mathematically generate examples of students who didn't want to go to college, forcing the model to learn the real differences rather than just guessing the popular answer.

"The most sophisticated model isn't always the most interpretable. We chose Random Forest not just for its 77% accuracy, but for its ability to reveal which factors truly mattered."
— Methodology approach

Several algorithms were tested, from simple baseline models to sophisticated ensemble methods. The progression was deliberate: starting with a dummy classifier that achieved only 50% accuracy, moving through Naive Bayes (69%), Logistic Regression (70%), and ultimately arriving at Random Forest and Decision Trees, which achieved our final ~77% accuracy rate.

"This analysis demonstrates exceptional rigor in both methodology and presentation. The use of SMOTE to address class imbalance and the comprehensive bias testing across demographic groups sets a high standard for educational data mining."

The Human Side of the Numbers

Behind every data point was a student with dreams, challenges, and choices. The analysis highlighted how interconnected these factors are. For instance, health status alone accounted for 15% of the predictive power, ranking as the fourth most important factor.

Perhaps most encouraging was the model's fairness across demographic groups, though some patterns emerged. While younger students were far more likely to attend college, male students showed a substantially lower likelihood of aspiring to higher education compared to their female counterparts. This suggests that gender-specific interventions might be necessary to bridge this gap.

Implications for Education Policy

These findings carry important implications for educators and policymakers. If attendance is indeed the strongest predictor of higher education aspirations, then interventions focused on reducing absenteeism could have profound effects on students' futures. This isn't just about punitive measures — it's about understanding why students miss school and addressing root causes.

The correlation with weekend alcohol consumption (18% importance) opens another avenue for discussion. While the model doesn't establish causation, it highlights the interconnected nature of student lifestyle choices and academic ambitions. Health and wellness programs that address the whole student, not just academic performance, may be more effective than traditional interventions.

Looking Forward

This analysis represents more than an academic exercise — it's a glimpse into the complex factors that shape young people's educational trajectories. The model's 77% accuracy suggests we can identify at-risk students early and provide targeted support before aspirations fade.

Yet important questions remain. The data comes from Portuguese schools in 2005-2006, and educational landscapes have evolved significantly. Social media, remote learning, and changing economic realities all play roles that this dataset couldn't capture. Future research should explore how these modern factors interact with the fundamental patterns we've identified.

What's clear is that educational aspiration isn't just about academic ability — it's about engagement, health, and the choices students make outside the classroom. By understanding these connections, we can build educational systems that support the whole student, not just test scores.
